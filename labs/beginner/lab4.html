Automata are mechanical devices designed to perform ‚Äúhuman/animal-like‚Äù actions. They usually serve a performative or artistic purpose and show a high level of craftsmanship and understanding of mechanism design. Karakuri („Åã„Çâ„Åè„Çä) is the Japanese term for automata, where the automata reflect stories or parts of Japanese culture like a ‚ÄúChahakobi Ningyou‚Äù which is an automata that bows as tea is being served.

In this lab, you will explore CAMs, gears, and linkages to design automata. First, you'll learn the principles through a lecture and answer design understanding questions.

Next, in pairs, you'll design a Bahrain-themed automaton, inspired by cultural elements like Hiya Biya, Ramadan, Eid, Gergaoon, palm trees, pearl diving...etc

In this lab, you will learn how to:

  • ‚úÖ Design CAMs, Gears, and linkages based on mechanical principles
  • ‚úÖ Design CAMs, Gears, and linkages in Fusion360
  • ‚úÖ Create Assemblies in Fusion360 for motion animation
  • ‚úÖ Design a Bahrain-themed automata from sketch, prototype, to final product

üî® Fabrication Quest of the Day

  • Today, you will CAD a Bahrain-themed automata that you will then lasercut. Your design guidelines are:
    1. Automata design must fit in a wood panel of XXcm by XXcm
    2. Your design must have a crank for manual actuation
    3. Use at least two motions via two different mechanisms (e.g., CAM and gear).
    4. Design a story and pick two motions you want by referring to the mechanism glossry tables ( Table-1, Table-2 ).
    5. Start with a shaft and add components.
    6. Base your design on Bahraini themes like Eid, Hiya Biya, or pearl diving.
    7. Your design should hide the mechanism from view from the end that users will see it, and be able to see mecahnsim from the other end
    8. Keep it simple; complex automata take too long.
    9. Think ahead of how you want to add color; we will do this next lab but think ahead!

    Part (6): Revolving Radially Symmetric Geometry(10 minutes)

    Video Summary:
    1. Revolve Command: Go to Create > Revolve in the toolbar.
    2. Select Profile: Click on the profile (the closed shape) that you want to revolve.
    3. Select Axis: Click the axis of symmetry (the cyliner axis we constructed) around which the profile will revolve.
    4. Set Angle: In the Revolve dialog, set the angle to 360¬∞ (or the desired angle for partial revolution if you want to play around with this!).
    5. OK: Click OK to complete the operation.

    Part (7): Making Hole for Keychain (5 minutes)

    1. Create a Tangent Plane: Create a plane that is tangent to the surface of your revolved part using Construct > Tangent Plane.
    2. Sketch a Circle on the Tangent Plane: Start a sketch on the tangent plane and draw a circle where you want the hole.
    3. Dimension the Circle: Use the Dimension tool to set the circle's diameter to 4mm (or your desired size).
    4. Extrude Cut the Hole: Use Create > Extrude, select the circle, and set it to Cut with the Symmetric direction to cut both sides of the part.
    5. Finish: Click OK to complete the hole creation process.

    Part (10): Assigning Materials & Colors (15 minutes)

    1. Assign Material:
      • Right-click the body/component in the Browser and select "Appearance".
      • Drag a material from the Appearance panel to the body (whole body or specific faces).
    2. Custom Color:
      • Right-click the applied material and choose "Edit".
      • Click the color box to select a custom color.

    Part (11): Assemblies in CAD (10 minutes)

    1. Ensure Blocks Are Components:
      • Convert blocks to components by right-clicking and selecting "Convert to Component".
    2. Create Assembly:
      • Click "Assemble" > "Joint".
      • Choose the Shaft as Component 1 and the first Block as Component 2.
      • Define the joint type as "Simple".
    3. Add Remaining Blocks:
      • For bodies, use "Align Relationships".
      • For components, apply "Joint" again.

    Part (12): Rendering (5 minutes)

    1. Switch to Render Workspace:
      • Click on the Design tab and switch to the Render workspace.
    2. Adjust Scene Settings:
      • Click on Scene Settings to adjust the lighting and background to your liking.
    3. Render and Save Image:
      • Click on In-Canvas Render to generate the render.
      • Once done, save the image to your computer.

    Part (13): Export for 3D Printing (5 minutes)

    1. Export the 3D Model:
      • Click File in the top-left corner, then select 3D Print.
    2. Use the 3D Print Utility:
      • Click on 3D Print from Utility.
      • Choose Application Custom, and then load CURA from your applications list.
    3. Open in Cura:
      • After selecting your model, click OK.
      • CURA will open automatically with the selected model.
    4. Export Each Component:
      • Repeat the process for each component to export them one by one.

    Part (14): Setting Up File for 3D Printing (10 minutes)

    1. Adjust Model Orientation:
      • Ensure your models are in the correct orientation.
      • Use Rotate and Move tools to adjust the position if needed.
      • Make sure models do not overlap.
    2. Adjust 3D Printing Settings:
      • Set Layer Thickness to 0.1mm (fine quality, for threading).
      • Set Speed to 100mm/s.
      • Set Fill Density to 15%.
      • Enable Support (set to "only touching the print bed").
      • Disable Build Plate Adhesion (usually recommend to use brim, but here we just don't want to affect your design).
    3. Slicing and Preview:
      • Click Slice to generate the G-code.
      • Click Preview to check your print setup before exporting.
