    Part (4): Designing CAMs by mechanical principles (20 minutes)

    CAMs affect two main aspects: üí® speed and ‚ÜïÔ∏è height, which you can design around.

    Designing CAM around Height:

    When considering height, two factors play a role: the shape of the CAM and the location of the shaft. Let‚Äôs first focus on the shape of the CAM, assuming the shaft is positioned at the center, then change the shaft location.


    üî¥ Part (A): CAM Shape
    • ‚ö™ A circular CAM produces no height variation. (think, why?)
    • ü•ö Changing the shape to an ellipse introduces height, where the height difference equals the difference between the radii.

    For example:

    If you need a design to move up by 3 cm and the smaller radius (1) is 5 cm, the larger radius (2) should be 8 cm (5 cm + 3 cm).


    CAMS can come in many shapes as you can see in Table-1, like triangles, hearts, and stars. Let's check your understanding now with a triangle CAM and the height difference corresponding to it.

    Checkoff [Check My Understanding]

    If I have a CAM in the shape of an equilateral triangle, how many heights does it have? Additionally, if I want the height to vary from 2 cm (h1) to 4 cm (h2), what would the side length of the triangle be?

    The location of the shaft also affects height. We will address this next.

    üî¥ Part (B): CAM Shaft Location
    • Centered Shaft: A circular CAM with its shaft positioned at the center produces no height change in the CAM follower.
    • Off-Center Shaft (Eccentric CAM): Moving the shaft off-center transforms the same circular CAM into an "eccentric CAM," which creates height variation.
    • Application to Other Shapes: This principle applies to all CAM shapes, such as ellipses. Shifting the shaft from the geometric center introduces height changes, even in shapes that already produce height variation when centered.

    ‚≠êExample‚≠ê: ramen noodles showing and hiding-- refer to Table-1 for more design refrences
    Placement of CAM & Effect on Rotation/Speed:
    Assuming that a CAM is circular, and its shaft is in the center, what would happen if the CAM is right below the center of the follower? What if it's a bit away from the follower's center?

    To understand how a CAM affects a follower, let‚Äôs examine three scenarios:

    1. CAM Directly Below the Follower's Center (1):
      • Circular CAM with Centered Shaft: No notable effects (may cause minor wobbling depending on constraints).
      • Elliptical or Eccentric CAM: Produces a change in height only, with little to no rotation (possible slight wobble).
    2. CAM Off-Center from the Follower ( 2 & 3):
      • The CAM causes rotation in the follower.
      • The farther the CAM is from the follower‚Äôs center, the easier it is to rotate the follower.
      • Why? The torque principle: Torque increases with the distance to the pivot.
    3. CAM Producing Both Height Change and Rotation:
      • If the CAM shape inherently produces height change (e.g., elliptical) and is placed off-center, it will result in both rotation and height variation simultaneously.

    Part (5): Designing Gears (Mechanical Principles) (20 minutes)

    There are many types of gears, which you can explore using this link. For our lab, we‚Äôll focus on spur gears and rack and pinion systems. For a summary of gear types and their applications, refer to Table-2.

    Gear Composition

    Gears (from a very high level) are "circular" discs with evenly spaced teeth around their circumference (you will see later that gears can in fact be non-circular).

    Gear Module

    Gears always come in pairs since they transfer motion/force. In order to have two gears mesh with each other (ie, the teeth engage and fit into each other), they must have the same gear module. The equation for the gear module is as follows (You can access additional equations here):

    Module (m) = OD/(N+2)

    where OD is the outer diameter, and N is the number of teeth

    So, if a gear has an OD (outer diameter) of 9cm, and has 16 teeth, then its module would be (9/(16+2)= 0.5cm), hence the module is 0.5cm

    Gear Ratio

    As mentioned earlier, gears come in pairs to transfer motion and force. One key reason for their use is to create mechanical advantage. For example, a gear can increase rotational velocity to be 2x or even 10x faster than its input (the first actuated gear). The actuated gear is called the "driving gear," and is either actuated by a motor or manually, while the gear that meshes with it is called the "driven gear." The relative speed of the driven and driving gears is referred to as the "gear ratio."
